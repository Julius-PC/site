Introduction

In January 2013, the Internet started off strong with the emergence of the “Harlem Shake” trend, its origins tracing back to a YouTube comedy sketch artist who boasted the moniker Filthy Frank. A deep dive into Filthy Frank’s other content revealed the true nature of this raunchy internet lowlife, with videos titled “RAT CHEF” or “RACIST WORDS IN JAPANESE (JAPANESE 101)” to name a few, the man behind the moniker being a Japanese American known as George Miller. George Miller would go on to receive a 5x Platinum RIAA certification 9 years later on his 2018 single “SLOW DANCING IN THE DARK” under his R&B persona, Joji. 88Rising, the representative mass media company for Joji and many more, is the mastermind behind such mainstream success in the late 2010s. Their distinctive approach in integrating their roster to a hip-hop and R&B audience in America allowed Asian- American talent to shine through the music industry, seemingly making the unmarketable marketable. This begs the question, how did a label that so meticulously formed the bridge between East and West, establishing a token brand that would seemingly take over the music industry, lose it all...?

Key Idea

Within a 5-year lifespan, 88Rising had gone from once taking on the Big Three in the Top charts, to completely dropping under the radar. Despite efforts to sustain their popularity, their downfall could very well be their own fault. 88Rising's shift in content towards a primarily Asian audience, capitalizing on the popularization of K-pop in international mass culture, significantly decreased traction among their previously broader demographic that celebrated their unique style, diminishing the company's overall commercial significance and media prominence.
