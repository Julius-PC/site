History of 88Rising

Founder and long-time CEO Sean Miyashiro had a clear vision for what he wanted the company to achieve as an Asian American brand. Before establishing 88Rising, Miyashiro was already involved in music. While working at Vice, a Canadian American lifestyle magazine, he started THUMP, the company’s electronic music platform that acted as a forum for news in music. “[Working] at THUMP taught Miyashiro invaluable lessons about brand-building in an ever-accelerating Internet culture”, translating over into the provision of his own independent company.1 The founder has often made remarks of people “likening [88Rising] to an Asian Vice in terms of content quality.”Unlike Vice, however, 88Rising was a medium "dedicated to celebrating global Asian culture". The only problem now was finding a way to reconcile the Asian American experience. As Miyashiro puts it, “We are clearly filling a void, which is a pan-Asian, East-meets-West approach.”

Born in San Jose, California to an Asian household, Sean Miyashiro’s plan to incorporate his two worlds originated from the multicultural melting pot of the Bay Area. Here, Miyashiro was introduced to a diverse range of musical genres growing up, taking an interest in the rising influence of hip-hop, particularly in the works of Wu-Tang Clan. The group’s name itself “[spoke] to [him], the way it infused appreciation and true knowledge” of Eastern culture into their music, Miyashiro claimed in an interview with Wu Tang’s own RZA. These sentiments carried on to how 88Rising would identify talent and focus their marketing, denoting an exchange of culture between Black and Asian.

The Star Roster

88Rising’s very first management signees were Korean and Indonesian rappers, Keith Ape and Rich Brian, followed by a Chinese rap group known as the Higher Brothers, and the aforementioned R&B act, Joji.1 The subsequent success of these artists in the U.S. helped to break into the wider Western demographic that appealed to these genres while subverting Asian stereotypes that had long protruded across music: Wiz Khalifa’s song “Hot Now” with lyrics “smoke got my eyes lookin’ Korean”, Doja Cat’s debut single “So High” with the line “now we both look Asian”, once again in reference to smoking, or the general fetishization of “yellow b*tches” and “Asian broads” across the rap genre.4 Instead, 88Rising revolved around paving the way for Asian artists to make meaningful music around their identity in the style of American music, sustaining a symbiotic relationship of East and West. “We want to push the culture forward,” Miyashiro tells CNN. “We’re not trying to break stereotypes or change people’s mindsets. We’re showing people what we can do, by doing what we do.”
